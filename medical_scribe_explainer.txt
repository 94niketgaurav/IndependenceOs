THE RUNNING EXAMPLE (used throughout this entire document)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  DOCTOR:   Dr. Sarah Martinez, primary care physician
  PATIENT:  John Smith, 61-year-old male
  VISIT:    Monday afternoon, March 14, 2026
  REASON:   John has had sharp chest pain for 3 days

We will follow this one visit from the moment the recording starts to the
moment the note is locked in the EHR and the bill is sent to insurance.

================================================================================
SECTION 0 — WHY DOES THIS SYSTEM EXIST?
================================================================================

After every patient visit, the doctor must write a "progress note." This is
not just a summary — it is a legal medical record AND the document that
determines how much the clinic gets paid.

A typical progress note has 13+ sections:
  - Chief Complaint (why the patient came in)
  - HPI (the full story of the current problem)
  - Review of Systems (head-to-toe question checklist)
  - Physical Examination findings
  - Assessment (what the doctor thinks is wrong)
  - Plan (what will be done about it)
  - Past medical history, surgical history, family history, social history
  - Allergies, medications, billing codes

Writing all of this takes a doctor 15+ minutes PER PATIENT.
A doctor seeing 20 patients a day = 5 hours of paperwork every night.
Many doctors finish notes at 10pm or later. Some take days.

This is the number 1 cause of physician burnout.
It also delays billing — the clinic can't get paid until the note is done.

THE GOAL: Record the conversation → automatically write the note → doctor
spends 2-3 minutes reviewing and approving it instead of 15 minutes writing.

SECTION 1 — STAGE 1: CAPTURE
  [Diagram nodes: Microphone → Consent Gate → Local Edge Agent →
   Audio Intake and Validation]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dr. Martinez walks into the exam room. She presses a button on a small
tablet, which says: "Recording will begin. Do you consent?" John nods and
says yes. Dr. Martinez confirms. Recording starts.

A small device in the clinic room — basically a smart microphone — picks
up everything that is said for the next 15 minutes. The entire conversation
is captured as an audio file.

BUT — before that audio goes anywhere, four things happen right there in
the room:

  1. NOISE IS CLEANED UP
     The device removes background noise — the AC humming, papers rustling,
     the door closing. Think of it like noise-cancelling headphones, but
     for the recording.

  2. SILENCE IS STRIPPED
     When Dr. Martinez steps out to check results and John sits quietly,
     those silent gaps are removed. This shrinks the file size by ~60% and
     makes everything downstream faster.

  3. THE AUDIO IS ENCRYPTED
     Before the audio travels anywhere over the internet, it is locked with
     AES-256 encryption. This is the same standard banks use. Nobody can
     read it without the key. This is critical because audio is extremely
     sensitive — it contains someone's voice, their medical problems, their
     personal details.

  4. IT IS SAVED LOCALLY FIRST
     If the internet connection drops, the audio is saved to the device.
     It will upload when the connection comes back. The recording never
     gets lost.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The LOCAL EDGE AGENT is a lightweight program running on a Mac mini or
tablet in the clinic. It runs:
  - RNNoise (real-time noise suppression library) or WebRTC for noise suppression
  - Voice Activity Detection (VAD) — silence detection and removal
  - Audio chunking into 4-second segments with 1-second overlaps
    (overlap prevents a word being cut in half between chunks)
  - AES-256 encryption with a per-clinic key from AWS KMS
  - An offline queue that flushes to S3 when network restores

AUDIO INTAKE AND VALIDATION receives the encrypted audio and does:
  - Format check: accepts WAV, MP3, M4A, FLAC, OGG
  - Normalizes everything to 16kHz mono PCM (the format ASR(Automatic Speech Recognition) models expect)
  - Duration check: rejects anything under 60 seconds (not a real visit)
  - Language detection: flags non-English for human review
  - Clipping detection: warns if audio is too loud and distorted

  IF AUDIO IS INVALID → goes to Alert Doctor → Dictation Fallback
  (Doctor speaks directly into mic, skips ASR/Diarization, jumps straight
  to Transcript Post-Processing. We know it's all doctor speech so no
  need to separate speakers.)

  IF AUDIO IS VALID → continues to Stage 2

SECTION 2 — STAGE 2: TRANSCRIPTION
  [Diagram nodes: Speaker Diarization + Medical ASR Engine (parallel) →
   Transcript Post-Processing → Clean Labelled Transcript]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Now the system does two jobs AT THE SAME TIME on the audio:

JOB 1: FIGURE OUT WHO IS SPEAKING
  The system listens to the voices and labels every sentence:
  - "So what brings you in today?" → DOCTOR
  - "I've had chest pain for three days" → PATIENT
  - "Any fever?" → DOCTOR
  - "No fever" → PATIENT

  This is important because the same words mean different things depending
  on who said them. When the PATIENT says "I've had chest pain for 3 days,"
  that goes into the Chief Complaint. When the DOCTOR says "Breath sounds
  are clear," that goes into the Physical Examination.

  HOW DOES IT KNOW WHOSE VOICE IS WHOSE?
  When Dr. Martinez first joined the clinic, she recorded 30 seconds of
  herself speaking. The system stored her "voice fingerprint." Every time
  a recording comes in, it matches voices to that fingerprint. Dr. Martinez
  = known voice. John = the other voice.

JOB 2: CONVERT SPEECH TO TEXT
  The audio is run through a speech-to-text model. But not a regular one —
  this is a MEDICAL speech-to-text model, trained on doctor-patient
  conversations. It knows words like "metoprolol," "pleuritis,"
  "atrioventricular" — words that regular speech-to-text often gets wrong.

  After both jobs are done, the transcription is CLEANED UP:
  - Medical spelling is corrected ("metropolol" → "metoprolol")
  - Filler words are removed ("um," "uh," "you know")
  - Sentences are properly separated
  - A safety check removes any patient name/ID that accidentally leaked

THE RESULT: A clean, labelled transcript that looks like this:

  [0:00-0:03] DOCTOR: "So what brings you in today?"
  [0:04-0:12] PATIENT: "I've had this chest pain for about three days now,
                         it's worse when I breathe in."
  [0:13-0:15] DOCTOR: "Any fever or cough?"
  [0:16-0:18] PATIENT: "No fever, no cough."
  [0:19-0:25] DOCTOR: "Any family history of heart disease?"
  [0:26-0:32] PATIENT: "My father had a heart attack at 60."
  [1:45-2:10] DOCTOR: "I'm going to listen to your lungs now.
                        Breath sounds clear bilaterally."

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

SPEAKER DIARIZATION uses pyannote.audio — an open-source speaker
diarization library. It uses a pre-enrolled voice embedding for Dr.
Martinez (stored at onboarding) and assigns every audio segment to either
DOCTOR or PATIENT based on cosine similarity of voice embeddings.

MEDICAL ASR ENGINE uses OpenAI Whisper large-v3 fine-tuned on medical
speech. Two boosts are applied:
  - RxNorm dictionary: 50,000+ drug names injected into the decoder beam
    search (improves medication recognition from ~71% to ~94%)
  - SNOMED CT terms: anatomy and procedure terminology

Both run IN PARALLEL on the same audio chunks.

TRANSCRIPT POST-PROCESSING runs five steps in sequence:
  1. Medical spell correction: Levenshtein distance + medical ontology
  2. Filler word removal: regex strip on PATIENT speech only
     (doctor speech is retained exactly — may contain clinical phrasing)
  3. Sentence boundary detection: fine-tuned BERT for punctuation restoration
  4. De-ID check: NER pass to catch any PII that leaked into transcript
  5. Conversation segmentation: groups turns into semantic topic blocks

OUTPUT: Clean Labelled Transcript — a JSON array of segments with
speaker, start/end timestamps, cleaned text, and confidence score.

SECTION 3 — STAGE 3: CLINICAL EXTRACTION
  [Diagram nodes: Entity Extraction → Section Population (13 parallel) →
   Consistency Check]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the BRAIN of the system.

The clean transcript is just a conversation. The progress note needs to be
a structured medical document with 13+ specific sections, written in
clinical language.

The AI (Claude) does this in THREE PASSES:

---

PASS 1: READ EVERYTHING AND PULL OUT ALL THE FACTS

  The AI reads the entire transcript once and makes a list of every
  clinical fact it finds. Like a student reading a case study and
  highlighting important points:

    Facts extracted:
    - John has had chest pain for 3 days
    - The pain is sharp and gets worse when he breathes in (pleuritic)
    - Severity: 7 out of 10
    - No fever, no cough, no blood in sputum
    - His blood pressure today is 142/88
    - He takes lisinopril 10mg daily for blood pressure
    - He is allergic to penicillin (causes a rash)
    - His father had a heart attack at age 60
    - Dr. Martinez listened to his lungs — they are clear
    - Dr. Martinez ordered an EKG and a D-dimer blood test

---

PASS 2: FILL IN EACH OF THE 13 SECTIONS

  Now, using those extracted facts, the AI fills in each section of the
  note. Each section gets its own focused task. 13 sections run at the
  SAME TIME to save time:

  CHIEF COMPLAINT section gets:
    → The AI writes: "Chest pain for 3 days."

  HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS section gets:
    → The AI writes: "John Smith is a 61-year-old male with a history of
      hypertension who presents with a 3-day history of sharp, left-sided
      chest pain rated 7/10, worsening with inspiration. He denies fever,
      productive cough, or hemoptysis..."

  REVIEW OF SYSTEMS section gets:
    → The AI lists every body system Dr. Martinez asked about:
      "Cardiovascular: chest pain present. Respiratory: shortness of breath
      on exertion. Gastrointestinal: denies nausea/vomiting..."

  FAMILY HISTORY section gets:
    → "Father: myocardial infarction at age 60."

  PHYSICAL EXAMINATION section gets:
    → "Auscultation: breath sounds clear bilaterally..."

  And so on for all 13 sections.

---

PASS 3: CHECK THAT EVERYTHING IS CONSISTENT

  The AI reads all 13 sections together and checks:
  - Does the Plan address every problem listed in the Assessment?
  - Do the billing codes match what is documented?
  - Is anything mentioned in the HPI (History of Present Illness) missing from Review of Systems?
  - Did the AI accidentally invent something not in the transcript?
    (This is called "hallucination" — a major risk with AI systems)

  HALLUCINATION GUARD (very important):
  Every single sentence in the generated note must be traced back to a
  specific timestamp in the original transcript. If the AI wrote something
  that has no matching line in the transcript — it gets flagged. The doctor
  can click any sentence and hear the exact audio moment that supports it.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

This uses Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 as the LLM orchestrator.

PASS 1 (Entity Extraction): Single prompt over the full transcript.
  Extracts structured JSON:
  {
    "symptoms": [{"text": "chest pain", "duration": "3 days",
                  "character": "pleuritic", "severity": "7/10"}],
    "medications": [{"name": "lisinopril", "dose": "10mg", "freq": "daily"}],
    "vitals": {"BP": "142/88", "HR": "96", "SpO2": "97%"},
    "allergies": [{"allergen": "penicillin", "reaction": "rash"}]
  }

PASS 2 (Section Population): 13 parallel Claude API calls, one per section.
  Each call receives:
  - The relevant extracted entities from Pass 1
  - The raw transcript segments classified to that section
  - Section-specific requirements (e.g. HPI must include: onset, duration,
    character, severity, location, radiation, modifying factors,
    associated symptoms)
  - A few-shot example of a well-written section

PASS 3 (Consistency Check): Single prompt with all 13 section drafts.
  Verifies cross-section consistency and grounding.

CONFIDENCE SCORING: Every extracted claim gets a score:
  0.9+  = explicitly stated → no flag
  0.7-0.9 = inferred with high confidence → subtle highlight
  0.5-0.7 = ambiguous → yellow flag, doctor must review
  < 0.5  = possibly hallucinated → red flag, mandatory review

SECTION 4 — STAGE 4: CODING ENGINE
  [Diagram nodes: ICD-10 Suggester + CPT Suggester (parallel) →
   Progress Note Assembler]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Medical billing uses two types of codes:

  ICD-10 CODES = "What is wrong with the patient?" (diagnosis codes)
    Example: I10 = hypertension, R09.1 = pleurisy

  CPT CODES = "What did the doctor do during the visit?" (procedure codes)
    Example: 99214 = moderate-complexity office visit, 93000 = EKG

These codes go on the insurance claim. They determine how much the clinic
gets paid. Wrong codes = claim denied = clinic doesn't get paid.

ICD-10 alone has 70,000+ codes. The system cannot guess randomly.

---

HOW ICD-10 CODES ARE PICKED (two-step process):

  STEP 1: SEARCH (fast)
    Think of this like a Google search over 70,000 codes.
    The AI takes the diagnosis text ("pleurisy confirmed on exam") and
    searches for the 50 most similar ICD-10 codes in a database.
    This is done using vector embeddings — turning the text into numbers
    and finding the closest numerical matches.

  STEP 2: PICK THE BEST ONE (smart)
    The AI reads the top 50 candidates and the actual clinical notes, then
    picks the MOST SPECIFIC applicable code.

    WHY SPECIFICITY MATTERS:
    "Chest pain, unspecified" = R07.9 → lower reimbursement, audit risk
    "Pleurodynia" = R07.81 → precise, supported by documentation ✓

---

HOW CPT CODES ARE PICKED:

  The most important CPT code is the VISIT LEVEL (called E&M code).
  This is the number that represents how complex the visit was:

    99213 = Simple visit  → ~$100 reimbursement
    99214 = Medium visit  → ~$150 reimbursement  (most common)
    99215 = Complex visit → ~$210 reimbursement

  The system scores the visit based on three things:
    1. How many problems did the doctor address?
    2. How much data did the doctor review? (labs, imaging, records)
    3. How risky are the treatment decisions?

  Score these three → get the E&M level.

  Other CPT codes come from the Plan section:
    "I'm ordering an EKG" → CPT 93000

---

THE RESULT: Both codes are generated, with confidence scores, and both
feed into the Progress Note Assembler which stitches the complete note
together: 13 sections + diagnosis codes + procedure codes.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

ICD-10 SUGGESTER:
  Stage 1: BioBERT (medical embeddings model) embeds the Assessment text.
  Cosine similarity search over a pgvector index of 70,000+ ICD-10 codes.
  Body system pre-filter reduces search space (e.g. "chest" → I00-I99
  circulatory codes only).
  Returns top-50 candidates.

  Stage 2: Claude receives top-50 candidates + assessment text and
  selects the most specific code with justification. Walks the ICD-10
  hierarchy tree and stops at the deepest level the documentation supports.

CPT SUGGESTER:
  E&M level: Scores MDM (Medical Decision Making) on three axes
  (problems, data, risk) → maps to 99213/99214/99215.
  Procedure codes: Pattern match from Plan section text.

COMPLIANCE GUARDS:
  - Upcoding prevention: LLM score < billed code → BLOCKING flag
  - Bundling check: CCI edits (code pairs that can't be billed together)

SECTION 5 — STAGE 5: DOCTOR REVIEW
  [Diagram nodes: Draft Note → Doctor Review Interface →
   Flagged Items Unresolved (loop back) → Doctor Signs]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

About 2-3 minutes after the visit ends, Dr. Martinez picks up her tablet.
The note is already written. She is not writing — she is REVIEWING.

The screen shows her 13 sections, already filled out. Green checkmarks
on the sections that look good. Yellow warning flags on sections where
the AI was less certain.

DR. MARTINEZ'S REVIEW EXPERIENCE:

  She taps "HPI" — reads the paragraph. Looks right. Taps "Accept."
  She taps "Review of Systems" — there is a yellow flag on one line.
    The AI wrote: "Patient denies hemoptysis" (coughing up blood)
    The AI is 68% confident John said this.
    Dr. Martinez clicks the sentence — the tablet plays a 4-second audio
    clip of John saying "No, no blood."
    She taps "Accept as-is."

  She taps "Plan" — there is a red flag.
    The AI wrote that she ordered a CT scan.
    But she never ordered a CT scan — she only ordered a D-dimer and EKG.
    This is a hallucination. The AI made something up.
    She taps "Delete" — the CT scan line is removed.

  She looks at the billing codes:
    R09.1 (Pleurisy) — 0.91 confidence. She taps "Accept."
    I10 (Hypertension) — 0.98 confidence. She taps "Accept."
    99214 (Level 4 E&M) — 0.88 confidence. She reviews — looks right,
    she had two problems to manage, reviewed data. She taps "Accept."

  She taps "SIGN NOTE."

  A confirmation appears: "2 flagged items were accepted without editing.
  Continue?" She taps confirm.

  The note is now SIGNED and LOCKED. Done in 2 minutes 47 seconds.

---

TWO RULES THAT CANNOT BE BROKEN:

  1. FLAGGED ITEMS CANNOT BE SKIPPED.
     The doctor must take an explicit action on every yellow/red flag.
     There is no "ignore all" button. She must Accept, Edit, or Delete.
     This is a legal and safety requirement.

  2. BILLING CODES ALWAYS REQUIRE EXPLICIT ACCEPTANCE.
     Even if the confidence is 0.99, the doctor must tap "Accept" on
     each code. Codes are never silently included.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

DRAFT NOTE is displayed with confidence scores per sentence.
Sentences below 0.70 confidence are visually flagged.
Sentences below 0.50 confidence are hard-flagged (mandatory action).

AUDIO LINKAGE: Every generated sentence stores source_segment_ids —
pointers to the transcript segments that support it. The UI retrieves
the audio clip for those timestamps on demand.

LOOPBACK: If Flagged Items remain unresolved:
  Flagged Items Unresolved → Doctor Review Interface (loop back)
  The doctor cannot proceed to signing until all flags are resolved.

SIGN & LOCK:
  1. Immutable snapshot of note is created (stored in DB, never editable)
  2. Doctor's private key signs the note (cryptographic signature)
  3. Audit log is finalized
  4. EHR (Electronic Health Record) push is triggered
  5. Billing submission is queued
  6. Note status → LOCKED

SECTION 6 — STAGE 6: OUTPUT
  [Diagram nodes: Signed Note → EHR Integration + Billing Submission →
   Epic/Athena/Cerner + Insurance Claims]
  [Error paths: EHR Dead Letter Queue, Billing Dead Letter Queue]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Once Dr. Martinez signs, two things happen simultaneously:

1. THE NOTE GOES TO THE EHR
   The electronic health record (the clinic's patient records system —
   could be Epic, Athena, Cerner, etc.) receives the complete note.
   John Smith's chart is updated. The next doctor who sees him will have
   this full visit record.

2. THE BILLING IS SUBMITTED
   The ICD-10 and CPT codes go to the insurance company (Blue Cross Blue
   Shield, Medicare, etc.) as a formal claim. The clinic will receive
   reimbursement based on those codes.

---

WHAT IF SOMETHING FAILS?

  If the EHR push fails (server down, network issue):
    - The system retries automatically (3 attempts, with delays)
    - If all 3 fail → goes to EHR Dead Letter Queue
    - The admin gets an alert and can manually re-export

  If billing submission fails:
    - Same retry logic → Billing Dead Letter Queue
    - Billing team is notified to resubmit manually

  IMPORTANT: These failure paths are completely separate from the
  Audio Error path earlier. An audio failure at the start of the pipeline
  never reaches billing. Billing is only ever reached via a signed note.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

EHR INTEGRATION: Assembles a FHIR R4 bundle:
  - DocumentReference resource (the note text)
  - Condition resources (one per ICD-10 diagnosis)
  - Procedure resources (one per CPT code)
  - Encounter resource linking everything

Connectors per EHR:
  - Epic: SMART on FHIR + Epic FHIR APIs
  - Athenahealth: REST API + webhooks
  - Cerner: FHIR R4 API
  - Fallback: CDA XML + PDF export

BILLING SUBMISSION: 837P EDI transaction to clearinghouse
(Availity or Change Healthcare), which routes to the correct payer.

FAILURE HANDLING: Exponential backoff retry (1s, 4s, 16s).
After 3 failures → dead-letter queue + admin alert.
Note status remains SIGNED even if EHR/billing push fails — the
legal record is preserved regardless.

SECTION 7 — THE ERROR PATH (What happens when audio fails)
  [Diagram: Invalid Audio → Alert Doctor → Dictation Fallback →
   re-enters at Transcript Post-Processing]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Suppose the microphone cable fell out mid-visit. Or there was so much
background noise that only 40% of the words were recognizable. Or John
spoke very quietly and the ASR got confused.

WHAT HAPPENS:

  Audio Intake and Validation detects the problem:
    "Audio confidence is 38%. Too low to process reliably."

  → ALERT DOCTOR: Dr. Martinez's tablet shows a notification:
    "Recording quality insufficient. Please dictate the note manually."

  → DICTATION FALLBACK: Dr. Martinez holds the tablet close and speaks:
    "Chief complaint: chest pain 3 days. HPI: 61-year-old male, sharp
    pleuritic chest pain, worse with inspiration, rated 7/10. No fever,
    no cough. Assessment: pleuritis, hypertension. Plan: D-dimer, EKG,
    ibuprofen 600mg TID, follow up in 4 weeks..."

  This dictation goes straight to TRANSCRIPT POST-PROCESSING.
  We skip Speaker Diarization (we know it's all the doctor speaking).
  We skip the Medical ASR with noise handling (the mic is clean now).

  The rest of the pipeline — extraction, coding, review — runs IDENTICALLY.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
KEY POINT FOR UNDERSTANDING THE DIAGRAM:
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The error path is a DEAD END that loops back INTO the pipeline.
It NEVER connects to EHR or Billing.

This is the most common mistake when reading the diagram — people see
the red lines and wonder if failed audio somehow reaches billing.
It cannot. The only way to reach billing is through:

  Audio/Dictation → Transcript → Extraction → Coding → Draft Note
  → Doctor Signs → Signed Note → Billing

There is no shortcut. Billing requires a signed note. Period.

SECTION 11 — THE THREE MOST IMPORTANT DESIGN RULES
================================================================================

If you remember nothing else from this document, remember these three:

  1. BILLING IS ONLY REACHABLE THROUGH A SIGNED NOTE.
     There is no path from "audio failed" or "processing failed" to
     billing. You cannot accidentally bill for a visit where the note
     wasn't completed and approved.

  2. FLAGGED ITEMS CANNOT BE SILENTLY IGNORED.
     Every item the AI was uncertain about must have an explicit human
     action. Accept, Edit, or Delete. No bypassing. This protects both
     the patient (clinical accuracy) and the clinic (billing integrity).

  3. THE NOTE IS IMMUTABLE AFTER SIGNING.
     Once the doctor signs, the note cannot be edited. Any corrections
     must be made as an ADDENDUM (a new entry that says "correction to
     the note from [date]"). This is a legal requirement — medical
     records cannot be silently altered.
