================================================================================
  INDEPENDENCE OS — END-TO-END PLATFORM EXPLAINER
  How Medical Scribe, Claim Scrubbing, and Care Gap Work Together
  Plain-English Walkthrough for Anyone Reading This for the First Time
================================================================================

This document tells one complete story. A patient named Maria Chen walks into
Dr. Patel's independent family practice for her annual wellness exam. By the
time she leaves — and in the weeks that follow — three interconnected systems
will have worked in sequence, each handing off to the next, to produce an
outcome that none of them could have achieved alone.

Read this after reading the individual explainers for Medical Scribe and Claim
Scrubbing. This document assumes you understand how each system works on its
own. Here we focus entirely on HOW THEY CONNECT and why those connections
matter.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE RUNNING EXAMPLE (used throughout this entire document)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  PATIENT:  Maria Chen, 58 years old
  DOCTOR:   Dr. Patel, independent family practice, Chicago
  VISIT:    Monday, March 16, 2026, 10:00 AM
  REASON:   Annual wellness exam + diabetic follow-up
  INSURANCE: United Healthcare

Maria was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes four years ago. She takes metformin
daily and sees Dr. Patel every three to four months. Her diabetes is generally
well-managed, but she has been busy lately and has not kept up with all her
preventive screenings.

Dr. Patel is a solo practitioner. He sees 22 patients a day. He does not have
a dedicated billing department — he has one office manager who handles
scheduling, billing, and patient communications. Every dollar that gets left
on the table due to a denied claim, or every patient who develops a preventable
complication, hurts the practice directly.

================================================================================
PART ONE: BEFORE THE VISIT
================================================================================

THE NIGHT BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT
---------------------------------

It is Sunday, March 15, 2026. Maria is already scheduled for tomorrow. The
Care Gap Detection system runs its nightly evaluation scan across all of Dr.
Patel's patients.

What is the Care Gap system doing at this hour? It is comparing what each
patient SHOULD have received against what they ACTUALLY received, based on
their age, diagnoses, and insurance plan. These standards come from HEDIS
quality measures — roughly 90 rules published by a national quality
organization (NCQA) that define exactly what preventive care each type of
patient needs and how often.

For Maria Chen, the evaluation engine checks every applicable measure:

  HEDIS Measure HbA1c Control (CDC-HbA1c):
    Rule: Diabetic patients aged 18-75 must have an A1C test every year.
    Last A1C result on file: January 4, 2025.
    Days since last test: 436.
    Status: OPEN GAP. Overdue by 71 days.

  HEDIS Measure Diabetic Eye Exam (CDC-EYE):
    Rule: Diabetic patients aged 18-75 must have a retinal exam every year.
    Last eye exam on file: October 12, 2024.
    Days since last exam: 519.
    Status: OPEN GAP. Overdue by 154 days.

  HEDIS Measure Influenza Immunization (IMA-FLU):
    Rule: All adult patients should receive a flu vaccine each season.
    Current flu season: 2025-2026. Vaccine received this season: None.
    Status: OPEN GAP.

Three open gaps. The Care Gap system now assigns a priority score to each gap
using a combination of factors: clinical severity (A1C overdue is high
urgency for a diabetic patient), how many days overdue, whether Maria is on a
Value-Based Care contract with United Healthcare (she is — which means Dr.
Patel gets a quality bonus payment if Maria's gaps are closed this year), and
whether there is an appointment coming up soon. That last factor is important.

The Care Gap system recognizes that Maria has an appointment tomorrow. It
immediately elevates all three of her gaps to HIGH PRIORITY, because having
an appointment in the next 24 hours is the single best moment to get a gap
closed. Studies show that care gaps are closed at three to five times the rate
during an active visit compared to outreach campaigns. The patient is present,
the doctor can order the service on the spot, and the service can be performed
in the same encounter.

The system prepares a pre-encounter summary for Dr. Patel.

MONDAY MORNING, 9:45 AM
------------------------

Dr. Patel opens his EHR fifteen minutes before Maria's appointment. The
Medical Scribe interface loads, and at the top of Maria's patient record he
sees a banner generated by the Care Gap system:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  OPEN CARE GAPS FOR MARIA CHEN — PLEASE REVIEW BEFORE ENCOUNTER
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  1. A1C Test — OVERDUE (last completed Jan 2025, 436 days ago)
     Action: [Order 82962 A1C Point-of-Care Test]
  2. Diabetic Eye Exam — OVERDUE (last completed Oct 2024, 519 days ago)
     Action: [Add referral to ophthalmology]
  3. Flu Vaccine — DUE THIS SEASON (not yet received)
     Action: [Administer VIS + vaccine today]
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Note: Closing gaps 1 and 3 today contributes to Dr. Patel's
  United Healthcare quality bonus target for 2026.
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This banner is the first integration point. Care Gap is talking to Medical
Scribe BEFORE the visit begins. Dr. Patel does not have to remember that
Maria is overdue for her A1C test. The system surfaces it for him at exactly
the right moment — when he can do something about it. The action buttons are
live: clicking the A1C button creates the order directly in the EHR.

Dr. Patel reviews the gaps and thinks: yes, I was going to check her A1C
anyway. I will order it today, discuss the eye exam referral, and offer her
the flu shot before she leaves.

================================================================================
PART TWO: DURING THE VISIT
================================================================================

10:00 AM — THE VISIT BEGINS
-----------------------------

Maria arrives. The Medical Scribe system is now recording. Dr. Patel taps
the START RECORDING button on his tablet and the conversation begins.

Dr. Patel is not thinking about documentation. He is thinking about Maria.
He asks about her blood sugar control at home, checks her blood pressure (138
over 86, slightly elevated), reviews her medication, and discusses how she
has been feeling. Maria mentions she has had some fatigue lately.

Meanwhile, the Medical Scribe system is processing every word:

  Step 1: Audio Capture
  The tablet microphone continuously captures the conversation in 30-second
  chunks. Dr. Patel and Maria are assigned as separate speakers (Doctor and
  Patient) using speaker diarization — the system can tell whose voice is whose.

  Step 2: Real-Time Transcription
  Each audio chunk is sent to a speech-to-air transcription model. Medical
  terminology is handled by a specialized medical language model that knows
  the difference between "metformin" (a diabetes drug) and words that sound
  similar but mean something entirely different.

  Step 3: Clinical Extraction (running in parallel)
  While transcription happens, a separate process is extracting structured
  clinical data from the transcript. It identifies:
    - Chief Complaint: Annual wellness + diabetic follow-up
    - Current Medications: Metformin 1000mg twice daily
    - Vitals (mentioned in conversation): BP 138/86
    - Subjective complaint: Fatigue
    - Physical exam findings mentioned aloud by Dr. Patel
    - The A1C test being ordered (captured as a planned procedure)
    - Discussion of referral to ophthalmology

10:35 AM — DR. PATEL ORDERS THE A1C TEST
------------------------------------------

Dr. Patel steps out briefly, and the point-of-care A1C test is run on a
finger-stick blood sample. Maria's result comes back: 7.4%. Well-controlled
for a Type 2 diabetic patient on metformin.

Dr. Patel returns, discusses the result with Maria ("Your A1C is 7.4%, that
is excellent, right in our target range"), and makes a note in his assessment
that diabetes is well-controlled. He also clicks the ophthalmology referral
button from the Care Gap banner, generating a referral order in the EHR.

The flu vaccine is administered by the medical assistant before Maria leaves.

10:42 AM — THE VISIT ENDS
--------------------------

Dr. Patel taps STOP RECORDING. The Medical Scribe system now has the complete
transcript. Within 90 seconds, it produces a draft progress note populated
into the EHR with all thirteen standard sections filled in. The note includes:

  Chief Complaint:
    Annual wellness examination and Type 2 diabetes follow-up.

  HPI (History of Present Illness):
    Maria Chen is a 58-year-old female with a history of Type 2 diabetes
    on metformin 1000mg BID presenting for annual wellness and diabetic
    follow-up. She reports fatigue over the past several weeks. Blood
    pressure is mildly elevated at 138/86.

  Assessment and Plan:
    1. Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, well-controlled.
       A1C 7.4% today. Continue metformin 1000mg BID. Referral placed
       to ophthalmology for annual diabetic retinal exam.
    2. Annual Wellness Exam.
       Age-appropriate preventive screenings reviewed. Flu vaccine
       administered today.
    3. Hypertension — monitor, recheck at next visit.

  Suggested ICD-10 Codes:
    E11.9   — Type 2 diabetes mellitus without complications
    Z00.00  — Encounter for general adult medical examination
    J10.09  — Influenza vaccine administration (for flu documentation)

  Suggested CPT Codes:
    99214   — Office or other outpatient visit, moderate complexity (E&M)
    82962   — Glucose, blood glucose monitoring device (A1C POC test)
    90686   — Influenza vaccine (quadrivalent, 0.5 mL dose)
    90471   — Immunization administration (flu shot administration)

Dr. Patel reviews the draft note. It accurately captures everything from the
visit. He makes one small addition to the HPI — he wants to note that he
counseled Maria on blood pressure monitoring at home — and then clicks SIGN.

The note is now locked in the EHR as a legal medical record. This action —
the doctor clicking SIGN — triggers the most important event in the entire
Independence OS platform.

================================================================================
PART THREE: THE NOTE IS SIGNED — EVENT CHAIN BEGINS
================================================================================

THE NOTESIGNEDEVENT
--------------------

When Dr. Patel signs the note, the Medical Scribe system fires an event called
NoteSignedEvent. Think of an event as a notification that something important
happened, carrying all the relevant details along with it. Every other system
in the platform that cares about a signed note is listening for this event and
responds the moment it arrives.

The NoteSignedEvent carries this payload:

  NoteSignedEvent {
    patient_id:       "MCH-2847"
    provider_id:      "PATEL-001"
    encounter_id:     "ENC-20260316-2847"
    signed_at:        "2026-03-16T10:51:22Z"
    icd10_codes:      ["E11.9", "Z00.00", "J10.09"]
    cpt_codes:        ["99214", "82962", "90686", "90471"]
    note_text_url:    "[signed note stored in EHR]"
    payer_id:         "UNITED-HEALTHCARE"
    insurance_plan:   "UHC-PPO-COMMERCIAL"
  }

Two systems receive this event simultaneously.

RECIPIENT 1: CLAIM SCRUBBING ACTIVATES
----------------------------------------

The Claim Scrubbing system receives the NoteSignedEvent and immediately begins
building a claim. It pulls together:

  - The ICD-10 and CPT codes from the signed note
  - Maria's demographic and insurance information from the EHR
  - The signed progress note text (for documentation audit)
  - United Healthcare's specific billing rules for this plan type

The claim moves through the five-layer scrubbing engine:

  Layer 1 — Structural Validation:
  Are all required fields present? Is E11.9 a valid ICD-10 code? Is 99214
  a valid CPT code? Are NPI numbers present and correctly formatted?
  Result: PASS. All fields valid.

  Layer 2 — Clinical Rules (CCI/NCCI/LCD):
  Does any CPT code bundle with another? Is 82962 medically necessary for
  a patient with E11.9? Can 99214 and the immunization administration code
  (90471) be billed together on the same day?
  Result: PASS. No bundling conflicts. Medical necessity established by E11.9.

  Layer 3 — Payer-Specific Rules (United Healthcare):
  Does UHC have special requirements for billing 99214 + wellness exam
  (Z00.00) on the same day? Yes — this is a known UHC policy: when a wellness
  exam and a separate E&M visit are billed on the same date of service, a
  modifier 25 must be appended to the E&M code to indicate it was a separate,
  distinct service from the wellness visit.
  Result: WARNING FOUND. 99214 missing modifier 25 for UHC same-day wellness.

  Layer 4 — Documentation Audit (LLM Layer):
  The AI re-reads the signed progress note and independently verifies that
  the codes are justified by the documentation. It confirms:
    - E11.9 is supported by the note ("Type 2 diabetes, well-controlled")
    - Z00.00 is supported ("annual wellness examination")
    - 82962 is supported ("A1C 7.4% today" — test was performed)
    - 99214 is supported by medical decision-making documented in the note
    - The modifier 25 issue from Layer 3 is confirmed: the note does describe
      a medically separate problem-oriented visit (hypertension monitoring,
      fatigue) in addition to the wellness exam, which justifies modifier 25.
  Result: Documentation supports all codes. Modifier 25 is clinically justified.

  Layer 5 — ML Denial Predictor:
  The ML model checks historical denial patterns for this payer/code
  combination. It notes that UHC has denied 99214 + Z00.00 on same day at
  an 18% rate when modifier 25 is absent. With modifier 25 present, the
  denial rate is under 2%.
  Result: MEDIUM RISK without modifier 25. LOW RISK with modifier 25.

The Issue Aggregator collects the results. There is one warning: the missing
modifier 25. The Auto Fix Engine reviews it. Modifier 25 is a billing modifier
(not a change to the clinical diagnosis or procedure) — this is within the
scope of automated correction.

The system auto-applies modifier 25 to CPT code 99214, changing it to 99214-25.
The fix is logged with a timestamp, audit trail, and the rule that triggered it.

The claim now passes all five layers. It is a clean claim.

The Claim Scrubbing system submits the clean claim to the United Healthcare
clearinghouse. This happens within four minutes of Dr. Patel signing the note.

RECIPIENT 2: CARE GAP RECEIVES NOTESIGNEDEVENT
------------------------------------------------

While Claim Scrubbing was running, the Care Gap system also received the same
NoteSignedEvent. It inspects the CPT codes in the event payload:

  CPT 82962 — A1C Point-of-Care Test: This maps directly to the open care
  gap for HbA1c Control (CDC-HbA1c). The test was ordered and resulted today.

  CPT 90686 + 90471 — Flu vaccine + administration: These map to the open
  care gap for Influenza Immunization (IMA-FLU). The vaccine was administered
  today.

The Care Gap system updates both gaps to PENDING CLOSURE. Why pending and not
fully closed? Because the NoteSignedEvent comes from the doctor's documentation
— it tells the system that the service was ordered and documented. But the
most authoritative source of closure evidence is the paid insurance claim,
which confirms that the service actually happened and was accepted by the
payer. The NoteSignedEvent triggers provisional closure. The ClaimPaidEvent
(which comes later) confirms it.

For the third gap — diabetic eye exam — no CPT code in today's visit covers
a retinal exam. The referral order was placed, but the exam itself has not
happened yet. This gap remains OPEN.

================================================================================
PART FOUR: THE CLAIM IS PAID — GAPS CLOSE
================================================================================

FOURTEEN DAYS LATER — MARCH 30, 2026
---------------------------------------

United Healthcare processes the claim. Because modifier 25 was correctly
applied, and the documentation was complete, the claim passes through UHC's
adjudication system without a denial. The explanation of benefits (EOB) comes
back with the following:

  Claim status:       PAID
  99214-25:           Allowed .00 | Paid .60 (80%)
  82962:              Allowed .00  | Paid .40 (80%)
  90686:              Allowed .00  | Paid .40 (80%)
  90471:              Allowed .00  | Paid .00 (80%)
  Total paid:         .40

The Claim Scrubbing system receives the Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA 835)
from the clearinghouse and confirms payment. It then fires a ClaimPaidEvent.

THE CLAIMPAIDEVENT
-------------------

The ClaimPaidEvent is the second major event in the platform chain. It carries:

  ClaimPaidEvent {
    patient_id:       "MCH-2847"
    encounter_id:     "ENC-20260316-2847"
    payer_id:         "UNITED-HEALTHCARE"
    paid_at:          "2026-03-30T14:22:10Z"
    claim_id:         "CLM-98271"
    paid_cpt_codes:   ["99214-25", "82962", "90686", "90471"]
    total_paid:       186.40
    denial_codes:     []
  }

The Care Gap system is listening for this event.

CARE GAP PROCESSES THE CLAIMPAIDEVENT
---------------------------------------

The Care Gap system receives the ClaimPaidEvent and runs the closure
confirmation logic:

  CPT 82962 paid by insurance: This is authoritative confirmation that the
  A1C test was performed and accepted. The A1C care gap (CDC-HbA1c) moves
  from PENDING CLOSURE to CLOSED.

  CPT 90686 and 90471 paid by insurance: Flu vaccine and administration
  confirmed. The influenza immunization gap (IMA-FLU) moves from PENDING
  CLOSURE to CLOSED.

  Result stored in the gap record: closed_at timestamp, closure_source
  (ClaimPaidEvent), encounter_id, paid CPT codes as evidence.

Two of Maria's three open gaps are now closed.

THE GAPCLOSED EVENT
--------------------

For each gap that closes, the Care Gap system fires a GapClosedEvent. This
event flows to two destinations:

  Destination 1: Reporting Dashboard
  The practice dashboard updates in real time. Dr. Patel can see that Maria
  Chen's A1C gap and flu vaccine gap are now marked as closed for the 2026
  measurement year.

  Destination 2: VBC Contract Tracking
  Maria's United Healthcare plan is part of a Value-Based Care contract with
  Dr. Patel's practice. Under this contract, Dr. Patel earns a quality bonus
  at year-end if his panel achieves certain HEDIS thresholds. Each gap that
  closes on a VBC patient increments the contract's quality score. The
  GapClosedEvent carries the measure ID, patient ID, and contract ID so the
  financial impact can be calculated precisely.

  For this visit: closing two VBC-eligible gaps for Maria Chen contributes
  approximately  in incremental quality bonus value toward Dr. Patel's
  year-end target (based on his contract terms with UHC).

================================================================================
PART FIVE: THE REMAINING GAP
================================================================================

DIABETIC EYE EXAM — STILL OPEN
--------------------------------

The referral to ophthalmology was placed during Maria's visit, but the
retinal exam itself has not happened. This gap is OPEN with a sub-status of
REFERRAL PLACED. The Care Gap system knows an action has been taken, but it
is waiting for evidence that the exam was actually completed.

What happens next is the outreach campaign.

Because the eye exam gap has a referral placed, the system does not send
Maria an outreach message saying "You need an eye exam — please schedule one."
She already has a referral. Instead, the system sends a more specific message:

  Outreach Channel: SMS (Maria's preferred contact method, on file)
  Message sent: Monday, April 6, 2026 — 10 days after the visit

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Hi Maria, this is a message from Dr. Patel's office.
  During your recent visit, Dr. Patel placed a referral for a diabetic
  eye exam with an ophthalmologist. This exam is important for monitoring
  your diabetes. Would you like help scheduling your appointment?
  Reply YES and we will send you the ophthalmology contact information,
  or call us at [office number].
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Maria replies YES. The system sends her the ophthalmology clinic information
Dr. Patel's office has on file. The gap sub-status updates to
PATIENT_CONTACTED.

If Maria schedules the appointment, the appointment scheduling integration
(when available) updates the gap to APPOINTMENT_MADE. When the
ophthalmologist completes the exam and files a claim, that claim's paid CPT
code (92250 — fundus photography, or 92014 — comprehensive eye exam) is
detected by the Care Gap system and the gap closes via the ClaimPaidEvent
pathway — just like the A1C gap, but at the specialist's practice rather than
Dr. Patel's.

================================================================================
PART SIX: THE DENIAL FEEDBACK LOOP
================================================================================

THE SYSTEM LEARNS FROM EVERY DENIED CLAIM
-------------------------------------------

Let us pause and look at a feature that operates invisibly throughout all of
this. The Claim Scrubbing system does not just scrub claims — it also learns.

When a claim gets denied (not in Maria's case today, but across thousands of
claims in the practice), the Denial Analysis Pipeline breaks down the denial
reason code and looks for patterns. If UHC denies claims with a particular
ICD-10 and CPT combination five times in a row, a candidate rule is generated
and submitted for human review. Once approved, that rule becomes part of the
scrubbing engine's payer-specific ruleset.

But there is a second, more subtle feedback loop that runs between Claim
Scrubbing and Medical Scribe.

The Medical Scribe system suggests ICD-10 and CPT codes at the end of every
visit, before the doctor reviews the note. These suggestions are generated by
a coding model trained on the visit transcript. Over time, the coding model
can be retrained using denial outcome data from Claim Scrubbing:

  - Claims that passed scrubbing and got paid = positive training examples
  - Claims that got denied due to wrong code suggestions = negative examples
  - Claims that required modifier additions = modifier prediction improvements

This means that the Medical Scribe system gradually gets better at suggesting
codes that will actually pass payer rules, not just codes that are clinically
accurate in isolation. The scrubber teaches the scribe.

For Dr. Patel's practice, this feedback loop has already improved the first-
pass acceptance rate on claims over the first 90 days of using the platform.
The scribe suggests fewer problem codes. The scrubber catches fewer errors.
Fewer claims need manual remediation.

================================================================================
WHY EACH SYSTEM NEEDS THE OTHERS
================================================================================

It is worth being direct about why this platform requires all three systems
working together, rather than any one or two of them.

MEDICAL SCRIBE ALONE
---------------------
Medical Scribe solves documentation. It gives Dr. Patel his 15 minutes back
per patient. The notes are accurate, complete, and generated in under three
minutes. But Medical Scribe working alone has blind spots:

  - It does not know which of Maria's care gaps are still open when
    Dr. Patel walks into the room. Without the Care Gap pre-encounter summary,
    Dr. Patel might forget to order the A1C test.

  - It suggests ICD-10 and CPT codes, but it does not know UHC's modifier 25
    rule for same-day wellness visits. The claim goes out with the wrong code
    format and gets denied. Dr. Patel does not get paid for two weeks and
    someone has to resubmit.

  - It cannot tell you whether the codes it suggested will result in a denial
    for this specific payer. It knows what happened in the visit. It does not
    know the payer rulebook.

  - It does not know that closing the A1C gap contributes to Dr. Patel's
    VBC quality bonus. The financial context is invisible to the scribe.

CLAIM SCRUBBING ALONE
----------------------
Claim Scrubbing catches billing errors and prevents denials. It is highly
valuable on its own — stopping 30% denial rates saves real money. But without
the other systems:

  - It operates after the visit. By the time it reviews the claim, the visit
    is over. If Maria's A1C gap was not addressed during the visit, the claim
    scrubber cannot retroactively make Dr. Patel order the test.

  - It does not know which gaps Dr. Patel was supposed to address. It knows
    that a claim was submitted. It does not know that Maria was overdue for
    two screenings or that a third gap still needs outreach.

  - It sends a clean claim to the payer and confirms payment. But it cannot
    tell you that the payment, multiplied across the whole panel, contributes
    to a specific VBC quality threshold. The financial context of quality
    measures is invisible to the scrubber.

  - Its denial feedback loop is powerful, but it only improves claim accuracy.
    It cannot improve clinical gap closure rates. Those require behavioral
    change at the point of care — which only the pre-encounter summary can drive.

CARE GAP ALONE
---------------
Care Gap Detection knows exactly who is overdue for what. It can send Maria
a text message reminding her to schedule her A1C test. But without the other
systems:

  - It can detect that an A1C test is overdue. But it cannot tell when the
    test has been completed unless it receives the data from somewhere. Without
    the NoteSignedEvent and ClaimPaidEvent, it has no idea the A1C was done
    during today's visit. The gap stays open. Maria gets another SMS reminder.
    Dr. Patel's quality score does not improve even though the service was
    delivered. He does not receive his VBC bonus.

  - It can show a pre-encounter summary, but without Medical Scribe embedding
    that summary into the doctor's workflow at exactly the right moment, the
    summary lives in a separate application that Dr. Patel may or may not
    remember to check.

  - It tracks gaps, but it has no way to know whether a billed claim for a
    service was actually accepted by the payer. A claim could be filed and
    denied, leaving the gap technically unverified. Only the ClaimPaidEvent
    from Claim Scrubbing provides authoritative confirmation.

ALL THREE TOGETHER — THE FLYWHEEL
-----------------------------------
When all three systems operate together, each one's output becomes another
system's input, and the cycle reinforces itself:

  1. Care Gap feeds context into Medical Scribe
     (pre-encounter summary → doctor addresses more gaps per visit)

  2. Medical Scribe feeds structured data into Claim Scrubbing + Care Gap
     (NoteSignedEvent → clean claim + provisional gap closure)

  3. Claim Scrubbing feeds payment confirmation into Care Gap
     (ClaimPaidEvent → authoritative gap closure)

  4. Care Gap feeds quality metrics into VBC reporting
     (GapClosedEvent → quality bonus tracking)

  5. Claim Scrubbing feeds denial intelligence back into Medical Scribe
     (denial patterns → better code suggestions → fewer future denials)

This is the flywheel. Each rotation improves all three systems. Care gaps
close at higher rates because the pre-encounter summary reaches the doctor
at the right moment. Claims pass on the first attempt because the scrubber
catches errors before submission and feeds intelligence back to the scribe.
Quality scores improve because gap closure is automatically tracked against
the claim data that confirms it. The practice earns more, documents less,
and serves its patients better — not because any one tool is extraordinary,
but because the three tools are connected.

THE THREE GOLDEN RULES OF INDEPENDENCE OS
================================================================================

These rules summarize the design philosophy behind how the three systems
connect. Every integration decision, every event schema, every feedback loop
traces back to one of these three principles.

  RULE 1: THE VISIT IS THE HIGHEST-VALUE MOMENT.
  -----------------------------------------------
  A care gap closed during a visit is three to five times more likely to
  actually happen than a gap closed through outreach. A claim submitted
  within minutes of a signed note is more likely to be accurate than one
  assembled hours later from memory. The entire platform is designed to
  make the visit moment count more — not just for the patient's health,
  but for the practice's billing accuracy and quality score.

  This is why the pre-encounter summary is the most important integration
  in the platform. Getting the right information in front of the doctor
  before the conversation starts is worth more than any amount of post-visit
  correction.

  RULE 2: EVERY EVENT IS AN OPPORTUNITY FOR ANOTHER SYSTEM TO ACT.
  ----------------------------------------------------------------
  NoteSignedEvent, ClaimPaidEvent, GapClosedEvent — these are not just
  notifications. They are structured data handoffs that allow one system
  to pick up exactly where another left off.

  The Medical Scribe system does not need to know anything about payer rules
  or care gap measures. It just needs to fire a well-structured NoteSignedEvent
  and let the other systems respond. The Claim Scrubbing system does not need
  to know anything about HEDIS quality measures. It just needs to fire a
  well-structured ClaimPaidEvent and let the Care Gap system respond.

  Good integrations are narrow and specific. Each system stays focused on
  what it does best. The event contracts are where the systems touch.

  RULE 3: THE SOURCE OF TRUTH IS ALWAYS THE PAID CLAIM.
  -------------------------------------------------------
  A doctor can document a service. A note can describe a test being ordered.
  A transcript can record the words "I am ordering your A1C today." But none
  of these is proof that the service was actually delivered and accepted by
  the payer.

  The ClaimPaidEvent is the authoritative moment. It means the insurance
  company reviewed the claim, confirmed eligibility, checked the codes, read
  the documentation, and agreed to pay. That is as close to ground truth as
  the healthcare system gets.

  This is why care gaps move to PENDING CLOSURE on the NoteSignedEvent (good
  evidence) but only move to CLOSED on the ClaimPaidEvent (authoritative
  evidence). The distinction matters for VBC quality reporting, where the
  bonus payment depends on having defensible proof that the service was
  delivered — not just documented.

  The paid claim is the receipt. Everything else is the intent.
