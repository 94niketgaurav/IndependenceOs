  DOCTOR:   Dr. Sarah Martinez
  PATIENT:  John Smith, 61-year-old male
  VISIT:    March 14, 2026 — chest pain + hypertension
  STATUS:   The Medical Scribe system just produced a signed progress note.
            Now the clinic needs to get paid for this visit.

The note contains:
  Diagnoses:  R09.1 (Pleurisy), I10 (Hypertension)
  Procedures: 99214 (Office visit, moderate complexity), 93000 (EKG)
  Insurance:  Blue Cross Blue Shield Illinois (BCBS-IL)

This combination of codes + note + insurance = a CLAIM.
The claim goes to BCBS-IL. BCBS-IL either pays it or denies it.

================================================================================
SECTION 0 — WHY DOES THIS SYSTEM EXIST?
================================================================================

About 30% of all claims sent to insurance companies get DENIED.
The clinic does not get paid. Staff have to investigate, correct, and
resubmit. Some claims are written off entirely — the clinic loses the money.

Here is the important part: MOST DENIALS ARE PREVENTABLE.

The errors that caused the denial were already in the claim before it was
ever sent. The insurance company found the problem. The scrubber's job is
to find that same problem FIRST — before the claim leaves the clinic.

Common reasons claims get denied:
  - A required field was blank (patient's insurance ID was missing)
  - Two procedure codes were billed together but insurance says they can't be
  - The progress note doesn't actually support the billing level claimed
  - The diagnosis code doesn't justify the procedure that was billed
  - Prior authorization was required but no auth number was included
  - The claim was filed after the insurance company's time limit

THE GOAL: Catch all of these BEFORE submission.
Move the denial rate from 30% → under 5%.

HOW: Run 5 layers of checks on every claim simultaneously, in under 10 seconds.

================================================================================
SECTION 1 — WHAT IS A CLAIM?
================================================================================

Before diving into the system, it helps to understand what a claim is.

A claim is a formal request for payment sent to an insurance company. It is
NOT just the progress note. It is a structured form (called CMS-1500 or 837P)
that contains:

  PATIENT INFO:    Name, date of birth, insurance member ID
  PROVIDER INFO:   Doctor's name, NPI number (unique provider ID), address
  PAYER INFO:      Insurance company name and payer ID
  VISIT INFO:      Date of service, place of service (office = code 11)
  DIAGNOSIS CODES: ICD-10 codes — what was wrong with the patient
  PROCEDURE CODES: CPT codes — what the doctor did
  CHARGES:         How much the clinic is charging for each procedure
  LINKED NOTE:     The progress note that backs up all the codes

The insurance company checks every single field against their rules.
Any mismatch = denial.

For John Smith's visit, the claim looks like this:

  Patient:    John Smith | DOB: 1965-03-12 | Member ID: XY123456
  Provider:   Dr. Sarah Martinez | NPI: 1234567890
  Payer:      Blue Cross Blue Shield Illinois
  Date:       March 14, 2026 | Place: Office (POS 11)
  Diagnoses:  R09.1 (Pleurisy), I10 (Hypertension)
  Procedures: 99214 - $185.00, 93000 - $55.00
  Total:      $240.00

SECTION 2 — STAGE 1: INPUT AND INTAKE
  [Diagram nodes: EHR System → Claim Builder → Raw Claim →
   Claim Intake and Parser]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Once Dr. Martinez signs the note, the clinic's billing system automatically
assembles the claim. Think of it like filling out a tax form — it pulls
the patient's info, the doctor's info, the codes from the note, and puts
them all into the standard claim format.

This "raw claim" is then handed to the Claim Intake and Parser.
The parser does two things:

  1. NORMALIZE THE FORMAT
     Claims can come in slightly different formats depending on which billing
     system the clinic uses. The parser converts everything into one standard
     internal format so all the scrubbing layers can read it consistently.

  2. RESOLVE RECORDS
     It looks up the patient record (confirms the insurance ID is active),
     looks up the payer record (confirms what rules apply to BCBS-IL),
     and links the progress note so Layer 4 can read it.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

EHR SYSTEM outputs an 837P EDI transaction (the standard electronic claim
format) or a JSON payload via API, depending on the EHR connector type.

CLAIM BUILDER assembles the 837P fields from:
  - Patient demographics from EHR
  - Provider details from practice settings
  - ICD-10 / CPT codes from the signed progress note
  - Charge amounts from the fee schedule

CLAIM INTAKE AND PARSER:
  - Normalizes to internal ClaimDTO (Data Transfer Object)
  - Resolves payer_id to the correct Payer record in the database
  - Fetches the linked progress note text for Layer 4
  - Runs a real-time eligibility check (confirms patient is covered today)

SECTION 3 — STAGE 2: THE SCRUBBING ENGINE (5 LAYERS IN PARALLEL)
  [Diagram: Scrubbing Engine group containing all 5 layers,
   all receiving input from Claim Intake and Parser simultaneously]
================================================================================

This is the core of the system. All 5 layers receive the claim at the
same time and run simultaneously. Total target time: under 10 seconds.

Think of it like 5 different specialists reviewing the same file at once:
  - A compliance officer (Layer 1)
  - A coding expert (Layer 2)
  - A payer policy specialist (Layer 3)
  - A documentation auditor (Layer 4)
  - A data analyst who has seen thousands of claims (Layer 5)

------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 1 — STRUCTURAL VALIDATION
  What it asks: "Is everything filled in correctly?"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAIN ENGLISH:
  Before checking whether the codes are right, check whether the FORM is
  filled out correctly. This is like proofreading a job application before
  worrying about whether the candidate is qualified.

  For John Smith's claim, Layer 1 checks:
  - Is John's insurance member ID present and formatted correctly? ✓
  - Is Dr. Martinez's NPI number valid? (There's a checksum on NPI numbers
    like a credit card check.) ✓
  - Do R09.1 and I10 exist as valid ICD-10 codes in 2026? ✓
  - Does CPT 99214 and 93000 exist in 2026? ✓
  - Is March 14, 2026 in the past (not the future)? ✓
  - Was the claim submitted within BCBS-IL's filing deadline?
    (Most payers require submission within 90-180 days of the visit.) ✓
  - Is the place of service code "11" (office) a valid code? ✓

  If any of these fail → BLOCKING error. The claim literally cannot be
  processed until the missing information is supplied.

TECHNICALLY:
  Pure rule validation. No machine learning. ICD-10 and CPT codebooks
  are loaded into a Redis set at startup. NPI validation uses the Luhn
  algorithm. Rules run as a validation chain. Each check takes < 1ms.

  Codebook freshness: ICD-10 updates every October 1. CPT updates every
  January 1. An automated job runs on those dates to flag any active
  claims using codes that were just deleted or changed.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 2 — CLINICAL RULES (CCI / NCCI / LCD / NCD)
  What it asks: "Are these codes allowed together? Does this diagnosis
                justify this procedure?"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAIN ENGLISH:
  This layer enforces published government coding rules. There are three
  types of rules here:

  RULE TYPE 1 — CCI EDITS (Can these procedure codes be billed together?)
    The government (CMS) publishes quarterly tables of CPT code pairs that
    CANNOT be billed together on the same visit without a special modifier.
    There are ~250,000 such pairs.

    Example (NOT from John's claim, but to illustrate):
    If Dr. Martinez billed BOTH 99214 (E&M visit) AND 20610 (joint injection)
    on the same day WITHOUT a modifier 25 on the E&M code — DENIED.
    The modifier 25 says: "the E&M visit was a separately identifiable service
    from the procedure." Without it, the payer assumes the visit is included
    in the procedure fee.

    For John's claim: 99214 + 93000 (EKG). This combination IS allowed
    together without a modifier. ✓

  RULE TYPE 2 — NCCI BUNDLING (Is one code already included in another?)
    Some procedure codes INCLUDE other procedure codes by definition.
    You cannot bill for both the whole AND one of its parts separately.

    Example: If you bill 27447 (total knee replacement), you CANNOT also
    bill 27310 (arthrotomy of the knee) — because opening the knee is
    already part of the replacement procedure.

  RULE TYPE 3 — LCD / NCD (Does this diagnosis make this procedure
                medically necessary?)
    Every CPT procedure has a list of ICD-10 diagnoses that justify it.
    If your diagnosis is NOT on that list — the payer says "not medically
    necessary" and denies.

    Example: If Dr. Martinez had ordered an MRI of the spine (CPT 72148)
    and billed it with Z00.00 (routine annual exam), it would be denied.
    An MRI of the spine is only covered for diagnoses like M54.5 (low
    back pain) or M51.16 (disc herniation) — not routine exams.

    For John: 93000 (EKG) is being billed. The diagnoses are R09.1 and I10.
    Pleurisy and hypertension are both on the LCD coverage list for EKG. ✓

TECHNICALLY:
  CCI data: CMS quarterly CSV files loaded into a hash map:
    {(cpt_a, cpt_b): modifier_required?} — O(1) lookup per pair.
  LCD data: CMS LCD database (~2,000 LCDs), loaded per MAC region.
  Rules stored in Rule Cache (Redis) for sub-millisecond lookup.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 3 — PAYER-SPECIFIC RULES
  What it asks: "What does THIS specific insurance company require
                that is different from the standard rules?"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAIN ENGLISH:
  Each insurance company has its own additional rules on top of the
  government rules. There are 1,000+ payers. Each has their own policies.
  These change quarterly with minimal notice.

  Examples of payer-specific rules:
  - "We require prior authorization before you can bill CPT 27447
    (knee replacement). You need to get our approval first."
  - "We only reimburse for CPT 93000 (EKG) if the patient has a
    cardiac diagnosis — R09.1 (pleurisy) is not sufficient by itself."
  - "Claims must be submitted within 90 days. BCBS's standard is 180
    days, but we have our own tighter window."

  For John's claim, Layer 3 checks BCBS-IL's specific rules:
  - Does 99214 require prior auth with BCBS-IL? No. ✓
  - Does 93000 require prior auth with BCBS-IL? No. ✓
  - Is there a BCBS-IL rule about pleurisy + EKG? No. ✓
  - Is the $240 total charge within the normal range for BCBS-IL?
    (Extremely high charges get flagged as possible data entry errors.) ✓

  All checks pass for John's claim.

WHERE DO THESE RULES COME FROM?
  Three sources, run simultaneously (called the Triple-Source Strategy):

  Source 1 — CLEARINGHOUSE FEED: Companies like Availity and
    Change Healthcare maintain pre-built payer rule databases covering
    ~600 of the top payers. These update automatically.

  Source 2 — PAYER PORTAL SCRAPER: A weekly automated job monitors
    each payer's provider portal website for policy updates. When changes
    are detected, a human reviewer approves the new rule before it goes live.

  Source 3 — DENIAL FEEDBACK: When a claim gets denied for a reason
    that wasn't in the rules database, that denial pattern is analyzed and
    a new candidate rule is created. Human reviews and approves it.
    Now the system knows about that rule. (More on this in Section 9.)

TECHNICALLY:
  Rules stored in PostgreSQL with effective_date and expiry_date.
  A claim for a DOS in the past uses the rules from that date —
  not today's rules. Critical for handling late submissions and appeals.
  Cached in Redis (per payer hash map) for fast lookup.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 4 — DOCUMENTATION AUDIT (LLM)
  What it asks: "Does the progress note actually justify what was billed?"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAIN ENGLISH:
  This is the most important layer — and the one no rule database can
  replace. It answers: does what the doctor WROTE support what the
  doctor BILLED?

  Remember from the Medical Scribe system: the codes were generated from
  the note. But could the codes be wrong? Could the note be too vague?
  Could the AI scribe have suggested a higher billing level than the note
  actually supports?

  Layer 4 reads the progress note and runs THREE checks:

  CHECK A — E&M LEVEL AUDIT (Did the note justify 99214?)
    The visit level code (99213, 99214, 99215) determines the bulk of
    reimbursement. To justify each level, the note must document a certain
    complexity of medical thinking. This is called Medical Decision Making (MDM).

    MDM has 3 components:
      1. How many problems did the doctor address?
         - John has: pleuritis (new problem needing workup) + hypertension
           (existing chronic illness). This = MODERATE complexity.
      2. How much data was reviewed?
         - Dr. Martinez ordered a D-dimer and EKG, and reviewed results.
           This = LOW-MODERATE.
      3. How risky were the decisions?
         - Ruling out a pulmonary embolism (PE) is HIGH risk.
           But overall treatment is moderate (NSAIDs, follow-up). = MODERATE.

    Score 2 of 3 components at MODERATE or higher → 99214 is justified. ✓

    If the note only supported 99213 but 99214 was billed → BLOCKING error.
    This is called UPCODING — billing for more complexity than documented.
    It is considered fraud. The system stops it before submission.

    If the note supported 99215 but only 99214 was billed → WARNING.
    This is DOWNCODING — the clinic is leaving money on the table.
    The system flags it so the biller can upgrade the code.

  CHECK B — DIAGNOSIS SUPPORT (Is every diagnosis code actually in the note?)
    R09.1 (Pleurisy) — Is pleurisy mentioned in the note? Yes. ✓
    I10 (Hypertension) — Is hypertension mentioned? Yes. ✓

    If a diagnosis code appears on the claim but is nowhere in the note →
    BLOCKING error. You cannot bill for a diagnosis that wasn't documented.

  CHECK C — PROCEDURE DOCUMENTATION (Was the EKG actually performed?)
    CPT 93000 (EKG) is billed. The note must say:
      - The EKG was performed (not just "ordered")
      - The interpretation/findings

    Dr. Martinez wrote: "EKG performed — normal sinus rhythm, no ST changes."
    Both boxes ticked. ✓

    If the note only said "EKG ordered" → BLOCKING. You can only bill for
    procedures you actually performed and documented.

TECHNICALLY:
  Three parallel Claude API calls, each with a structured JSON output:

  Call A: MDM scoring prompt → returns {problems_score, data_score,
    risk_score, supported_em_level, match, reasoning, confidence}

  Call B: Diagnosis support prompt → for each ICD-10 code, returns
    {code, supported: bool, evidence: "quote from note"}

  Call C: Procedure documentation prompt → for each CPT, returns
    {code, performed_documented: bool, findings_documented: bool, evidence}

  Every finding must include an evidence quote from the note.
  If confidence < 0.70, result escalates to senior biller for human review.
  If confidence < 0.50, item is hard-flagged as uncertain.

  HALLUCINATION GUARD: Same as Medical Scribe — if LLM returns a finding
  with no evidence quote, it is discarded and sent to human review.

NOTE AMENDMENT LOOP:
  If Layer 4 finds that the EKG was "ordered not performed," the system
  sends a note amendment request to Dr. Martinez:
  "Please add EKG interpretation findings to your note for today's visit."
  Dr. Martinez adds an addendum. Layer 4 re-runs on the updated note.
  If it now passes → the issue is resolved → claim continues to submission.

  IMPORTANT: The note amendment loop is shown in the diagram as a DASHED
  yellow arrow going BACKWARD from Note Amendment Request to Layer 4.
  This is the only backward arrow in the entire flow.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
LAYER 5 — ML DENIAL PREDICTOR
  What it asks: "Even if everything looks right, is this claim
                likely to get denied based on historical patterns?"
------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAIN ENGLISH:
  This layer is different from the other four. Layers 1-4 check KNOWN
  rules. Layer 5 looks at PATTERNS — things that have gotten denied before
  even when the claim looked correct.

  Think of it like a colleague who has worked with BCBS-IL for 5 years
  saying: "I don't know why, but they always deny knee replacements billed
  on Fridays. Just avoid Fridays." The rule isn't published anywhere.
  It's just a pattern that experience reveals.

  Layer 5 is a machine learning model trained on tens of thousands of
  past claims and their outcomes (paid or denied). It looks at:
  - This exact combination of ICD-10 + CPT codes
  - This specific payer
  - The provider's history with this payer
  - Whether prior auth is present
  - The dollar amount of the claim

  It outputs a DENIAL PROBABILITY:
    0.13 → "Low risk, looks fine"
    0.45 → "Elevated risk — here's why"
    0.73 → "High risk — review before submitting"

  For John's claim: 0.18. Low risk. No flag.

  BUT — Layer 5 never produces a BLOCKING error. It is ADVISORY ONLY.
  We cannot stop a claim just because the model thinks it might get denied.
  A claim that looks 100% correct can still get a 0.73 risk score because
  the payer has been quirky lately. The biller sees the score and decides.

  HOW IT EXPLAINS ITSELF:
  The model uses SHAP values — a technique that shows which factors
  contributed most to the score. The biller sees something like:
    "Denial probability: 73%
     Top reasons:
       +31% — This CPT+diagnosis pair denied 68% of the time with this payer
       +22% — No prior auth on file
       +12% — Claim amount in 94th percentile for this payer"

  Now the biller has actionable information, not just a scary number.

TECHNICALLY:
  Model: LightGBM (gradient boosting) — fast inference (sub-millisecond),
  handles sparse categorical features, interpretable via SHAP.
  Retrained monthly on rolling 12-month window of paid/denied outcomes.
  Minimum 500 claims per payer before payer-specific model activates.
  Below threshold → uses specialty-level aggregate model.

SECTION 4 — STAGE 3: ISSUE AGGREGATOR AND PRIORITIZER
  [Diagram node: Issue Aggregator and Prioritizer]
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

All 5 layers have finished and each has produced a list of issues they found.
The aggregator collects ALL issues from ALL layers and sorts them by severity:

  BLOCKING (❌):  The claim WILL be denied if submitted as-is.
                  Must be fixed before submission is allowed.
                  Examples: missing modifier, unsupported diagnosis,
                  procedure documented as "ordered not performed."

  WARNING (⚠️):  The claim has elevated denial risk.
                  The biller must acknowledge each warning before submitting.
                  They can choose to proceed, but they have to consciously
                  do so — not accidentally.
                  Examples: ML risk score > 40%, possible downcoding detected.

  INFO (ℹ️):     Advisory only. Can be dismissed with one click.
                  Examples: "Consider a more specific diagnosis code."

For John's claim, suppose the aggregator finds:
  ❌ BLOCKING — No modifier 25 on 99214, which was billed with 93000
                (the EKG was performed during the same visit — CCI edit)
  ⚠️ WARNING  — ML denial probability 18% (low — but reported for visibility)
  ✓ PASS     — All other checks passed

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Issues from all 5 layers arrive in a results queue. The aggregator:
  - Deduplicates issues (two layers might catch the same thing)
  - Assigns final severity (BLOCKING > WARNING > INFO)
  - Groups related issues (same CPT code pair showing up in Layer 2 and 3)
  - Calculates overall claim status: CLEAN / HAS_WARNINGS / HAS_BLOCKING
  - Generates suggested_fix text for each issue

WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The billing specialist opens the claim on their screen. They see the

The biller clicks "Auto-fix." The system adds modifier 25 to the 99214

  99214-25 (the "-25" is the modifier) — $185.00
  93000                                 — $55.00

The insurance company now knows: yes, we know the EKG was the main
procedure, AND the E&M visit was a separate and identifiable service.
Both are billable. No denial.

---

THREE PATHS THROUGH REMEDIATION:

PATH 1 — AUTO-FIX (no human needed):
  For issues that are purely formatting/coding corrections:
  - Add a missing modifier
  - Re-order diagnosis codes (primary diagnosis must be first)
  - Remove a duplicate claim
  The system fixes these automatically. Biller just reviews the change log.

  HARD RULE: Auto-fix ONLY changes billing/coding fields (modifiers,
  code order, formatting). It NEVER changes the actual diagnosis codes
  or procedure codes. Those require human approval. Changing clinical
  content without doctor sign-off is a compliance violation.

PATH 2 — MANUAL REVIEW QUEUE (biller judgment call):
  For issues requiring human decision:
  - Prior authorization missing — biller must contact payer to get auth
  - ML risk warning — biller decides whether to hold or proceed
  - Payer-specific rule — biller verifies with payer portal directly

PATH 3 — NOTE AMENDMENT REQUEST (doctor involvement):
  For issues where the progress note itself is the problem:
  - "EKG documented as ordered, not performed" — doctor must add findings
  - "Diagnosis I10 not mentioned in note" — doctor must add documentation

  THE AMENDMENT LOOP (the only backward arrow in the diagram):
    1. System sends amendment request to Dr. Martinez's queue
    2. Dr. Martinez adds an addendum (original note stays immutable)
    3. Layer 4 Documentation Audit RE-RUNS on the updated note
    4. If Layer 4 now passes → issue resolved → continues to submission
    5. If Layer 4 still fails → back to Remediation Interface

  CRITICAL: Note Amendment does NOT jump directly to the submission gate.
  It must re-run Layer 4. If we allowed a direct shortcut, we could accept
  a note amendment that still doesn't actually fix the problem.

---

THE BLOCKING ISSUE LOOP:
  As long as ANY blocking issue remains unresolved, the claim cannot proceed.
  The Remediation Interface loops back on itself until every blocking issue
  is resolved.

WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

All blocking issues are resolved. All warnings have been acknowledged.
The biller clicks "Submit."

The claim — now corrected — is transmitted through a CLEARINGHOUSE.
A clearinghouse is a middleman company (like Availity or Change Healthcare)
that:
  1. Validates the 837P file format one more time
  2. Routes the claim to the correct insurance company

The insurance company (BCBS-IL) receives the claim.

For John's claim:
  Before scrubbing: 99214 + 93000 — would have been denied (no modifier)
  After scrubbing:  99214-25 + 93000 — properly formatted, should be paid

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IF THE CLEARINGHOUSE IS DOWN?
------------------------------------------------------------------------

  If the connection to the clearinghouse fails:
  → Claim goes to LOCAL CLAIM QUEUE (stored locally)
  → System retries automatically when connection is restored
  → Biller is notified if retry fails after 3 attempts

  [Diagram: red dashed arrow from Clearinghouse → Clearinghouse Down →
   Local Claim Queue]

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (technically):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

CLEAN CLAIM is assembled as a valid 837P EDI transaction with all
corrections applied. The audit log records every change made during
remediation (auto-fix or manual).

CLEARINGHOUSE receives the 837P, runs its own format validation (a
final safety net), then routes to the payer-specific endpoint.

PAYER processes the claim against their adjudication rules and
responds with an 835 ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice) file.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT HAPPENS WHEN A CLAIM IS DENIED?
------------------------------------------------------------------------

A denied claim does NOT just disappear. It enters the FEEDBACK LOOP.
(See Section 8 — this is what makes the system smarter over time.)

Options for a denied claim:
  1. CORRECT AND RESUBMIT: Fix the error, submit a corrected claim
  2. APPEAL: Formally dispute the denial with the payer
  3. WRITE OFF: If the denial is valid and uncorrectable, absorb the loss

SECTION 8 — STAGE 7: THE FEEDBACK LOOP

------------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS HAPPENING (plain English):
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every denial that comes back is a lesson. The feedback loop captures
that lesson and makes the scrubber smarter.

SCENARIO: Suppose John's claim was denied with reason OA-23
("Prior authorization not obtained") for CPT 93000 (EKG) from BCBS-IL.
The scrubber missed this because there was no rule in the database saying
"BCBS-IL requires prior auth for ECGs on patients with pleurisy only."
That's a hyper-specific payer rule that was never in any published document.

HERE IS WHAT HAPPENS:

  STEP 1 — DENIAL ANALYSIS
    The denial reason code (OA-23) is parsed from the 835 ERA file.
    The system looks at the claim that was denied: what codes, what payer,
    what reason.

  STEP 2 — PATTERN DETECTION
    It asks: "Have we seen this before?"
    It finds: in the last 90 days, 14 other claims with CPT 93000 billed
    to BCBS-IL were denied with OA-23. That's a pattern.

  STEP 3 — CANDIDATE RULE CREATED
    The system generates a candidate payer rule:
      "BCBS-IL requires prior auth for CPT 93000.
       Source: Denial feedback. Confidence: 0.82."

  STEP 4 — HUMAN REVIEW
    This candidate rule is sent to the billing manager for approval.
    They verify it against the BCBS-IL provider portal. Confirmed — BCBS-IL
    did quietly add a prior auth requirement in their January update.

  STEP 5 — RULE ACTIVATED
    The approved rule is added to the Rule Store.
    From now on, every claim with BCBS-IL + CPT 93000 will get a
    Layer 3 BLOCKING flag: "Prior auth required — get auth before billing."

    No future claim will be denied for this reason.

---

SEPARATELY — ML MODEL RETRAINING:
  ALL denials (not just new patterns) feed into the ML model retraining.
  Monthly, the model is retrained on the updated dataset.
  It gets better at predicting denials — including subtle patterns
  that don't generate a discrete new rule.

SECTION 10 — HOW CLAIM SCRUBBING CONNECTS TO MEDICAL SCRIBE
================================================================================

These two systems are not separate — they are a continuous pipeline.

  MEDICAL SCRIBE:
    Audio → Transcript → Clinical Extraction → Coding → Doctor Signs Note

  CLAIM SCRUBBING (picks up here):
    Signed Note → Claim Builder → Scrubbing → Billing

The ICD-10 and CPT codes that the Medical Scribe suggested are the SAME
codes that the Claim Scrubber checks. If the scribe makes a coding mistake
(suggests the wrong E&M level, or misses a required modifier), the
scrubber catches it before money is lost.

This is why Layer 4 (Documentation Audit) is so valuable — it is
specifically designed to catch errors that the Medical Scribe system
might have introduced. It re-reads the progress note and independently
verifies that the codes are justifiable.

Think of Medical Scribe as the WRITER and Claim Scrubbing as the EDITOR.
Both need to be correct for the clinic to get paid.

SECTION 12 — THE THREE MOST IMPORTANT RULES
================================================================================

  1. EVERY CLAIM MUST PASS THE SUBMISSION GATE.
     "All Blocking Issues Resolved" is the ONLY entry point to Clean Claim.
     There is no way to submit a claim with an unresolved blocking issue.
     Auto-fixes, manual resolutions, and note amendments all route through
     this gate. No shortcuts.

  2. AUTO-FIX NEVER TOUCHES CLINICAL CONTENT.
     Modifiers, code order, and formatting can be auto-fixed.
     Actual diagnosis codes (ICD-10) and procedure codes (CPT) require
     explicit human approval. Changing clinical data without authorization
     is a compliance violation — potentially fraud.

  3. THE NOTE AMENDMENT LOOP ALWAYS RE-RUNS LAYER 4.
     When a doctor amends their note to fix a documentation gap, the system
     does NOT simply trust that the amendment fixed the problem. It re-runs
     the full documentation audit (Layer 4) and checks again. Only if Layer 4
     passes does the issue get resolved. This prevents "sham amendments"
     that don't actually address the coding issue.
